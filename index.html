Democratic Executive
Committee Manual

2026  ·  Version 1.0  ·  Florida Democratic Party

Florida is a diverse state with large and small counties, urban and rural counties, predominantly Democratic counties, and counties where Democrats are heavily outnumbered. No set of standardized practices can be uniformly applied to such a diverse network.

However, Democratic Executive Committees (DECs) in all counties do have common duties and responsibilities as established by Florida Statute, as well as common expectations as established by the Florida Democratic Party (FDP), and each county is a critical part of the state's success.

This manual addresses common duties and expectations and promotes best practices found in some of Florida's most successful DECs. It addresses requirements established by Florida law and by FDP bylaws, as well as operating rules vital to the FDP's continuity and efficiency.

DECs are a crucial part of a statewide party infrastructure that often needs to act with speed, consistency, and uniformity to achieve the goal of electing Federal, State, and local Democrats throughout Florida.

Living Document: This Handbook will be updated and improved as needs change and circumstances warrant. DEC chairs will make the handbook available to their membership.

A large group of invigorated, well-trained grassroots Democrats is one of Florida's very best resources. We recommend this handbook be widely dispersed among all DEC members so everyone can be kept well informed about the DEC's job and how it fits into the larger picture.

Five Important Principles of the DECs

1
It's About the DECs!

Within the context of the DEC, none of us is more important than our common objective.

2
Mutual Respect

Maintain a culture of mutual respect, keeping personal bickering to an absolute minimum.

3
Innovation

Smart, creative ideas are the key to success.

4
High Expectations

We will have high expectations of each other, understanding that everyone is fallible.

5
Openness

Everything we do — meetings, voting, documentation, finances — will be open to all members.

As Tip O'Neill said, "All politics is local." Our activism starts in town halls and community centers, and our energy comes from a multitude of diverse interests and opinions. Our grassroots nature calls for a wide array of committees, clubs, and caucuses.

What is a DEC?

A DEC is an official county arm of the FDP. The DEC shall have oversight of all Democratic Party activities within the county. It shall carry out all directives granted to it by the FDP and by Florida statutes.

As part of this effort, the DEC shall:

  1. Effectively support the election of Democrats
  2. Create an organizational 5–10 year strategic plan, updating every 2 years
  3. Create an election plan for every election cycle and modify as needed
  4. Advance the visibility and viability of the Democratic Party within the county
  5. Register Voters in Democrat-leaning areas regularly
  6. Increase the number of Vote-by-Mail requests for Democrats and like-minded voters
  7. Ensure Democratic Party values and initiatives are effectively advanced within the county
  8. Engage as much as possible in local county affairs of interest to county Democrats
  9. Communicate with the public regarding elections and Democratic candidates
Precinct/District Committeepersons make up the majority of a DEC's membership. Elections for these positions are held every four years during the primary election cycle of every Presidential election year.

Mandatory DEC Activities

Several activities are required of a county DEC to ensure every effort is made to win elections at all levels:

  1. Recruit, train, and retain volunteers including Precinct/District Committeepersons by creating a welcoming, energizing space
  2. Register Voters
  3. Encourage Vote-by-Mail enrollment
  4. Fundraise for DEC operations, campaign initiatives, local candidates, and GOTV efforts
  5. Turnout of the Democratic base in all elections held within the county
  6. Utilize all voter outreach tools, including VAN, phone banking, texting, postcarding, emailing, and social media
  7. Recruit candidates
  8. Establish a voter protection team to ensure elections are run fairly
  9. Ensure compliance with State Statutes and FDP/DEC bylaws

Types of DEC Membership

Defined by Florida Statutes, Chapter 103.091 and FDP Bylaws, Article V, Section 1:

1. Elected Members (Precinct / District Committeepersons)
Chosen in the presidential election-year primary to represent a precinct or district. Take office on the first day of the month following the presidential general election and serve a four-year term.
2. Automatic Members
Members by virtue of Florida Statutes or DEC bylaw — county Democratic elected officials and presidents of duly chartered clubs or local caucus chapters who reside in the county. Have voting privileges but may not serve as officers.
3. Appointed (At-large) Members
Appointed by the County DEC Chair and approved by majority vote. Limited to no more than 10% of the county's total elected membership. Have voting privileges but may not serve as officers.
4. Special At-Large Members
At any time after election of officers, a county DEC may allow special at-large status for a Democratic Party elected official. Not included in the appointed membership quota.

Precinct Systems

Standard Precinct System

The default setup: elected DEC membership is organized by precinct, with one man and one woman elected from each precinct. As precinct Democratic registration grows, the bylaws add capacity — each precinct earns an additional man and woman for every additional 1,000 registered Democrats.

Optional Precinct System

In counties with fewer than 200 precincts, bylaws allow an "optional" precinct approach that still keeps representation rooted in precincts, but gives the county flexibility to add members to cover precincts that exceed 1,000 registered voters.

District System

In counties with more than 100 precincts, a DEC can choose — by majority vote taken before December 31 in the year before a presidential-election year — to elect members by "election districts" instead of individual precincts. One man and one woman per district, with additional seats added as the district's Democratic registration exceeds 1,000.

DEC Members

Every DEC member should be actively engaged in party activities by:

  • Supporting their precinct/district plans and county campaign plans
  • Playing an active role in Get Out The Vote (GOTV), Early Voting, and Vote-by-Mail programs
  • Attending all DEC meetings in person or by proxy
  • Assisting in filling vacancies in precinct/district committee offices
  • Facilitating the distribution of all Democratic campaign literature
  • Assisting in fundraising activities of the DEC
  • Taking and abiding by the Party loyalty oath
  • Serving on one of the DEC standing committees
  • Participating in Election Day activities such as providing rides to the polls, poll watching, canvassing, and phone banking
DEC Chair

The leader and presiding officer of the official county arm of the FDP. Responsible for supporting the election of Democrats at all levels, executing FDP policies, and maintaining operational, legal, and bylaw compliance. Acts as the official spokesperson for the Democratic Party within the county.

DEC Vice Chair (First Vice Chair)

An elected officer and member of the Steering Committee. In the absence of the Chair, the Vice Chair assumes their duties. Serves as an ex-officio member of all committees.

DEC Secretary

Keeper of records. Key responsibilities include:

  • Take, record, and present minutes at all DEC meetings
  • Forward minutes to Party Affairs within 20 days of approval
  • Send notice of a general meeting at least 10 days prior
  • Send membership list changes to Party Affairs and SOE within 10 days
  • Serve on credentials/membership committee
DEC Treasurer

Chief financial officer for the local party. Key responsibilities include:

  • Deposit all DEC funds in designated bank(s)
  • Keep accurate records of all receipts and expenditures
  • Provide an accounting at each DEC meeting
  • File all required reports with the Supervisor of Elections (SOE)
  • Arrange an annual financial audit and file with FDP and SOE by April 1

State Committee Members

Facilitate communication between the state and local party; take issues from the DEC to the FDP; report FDP business to the DEC; keep DEC informed of all changes in the Charter and Bylaws; and attend all State Executive Committee meetings.

DECs meet regularly throughout the year to discuss responsibilities, challenges, and issues facing Democrats at all levels. While a quarterly meeting is sufficient for FDP minimum requirements, best practice is a regular monthly meeting, ideally held on a consistent date and in a consistent, accessible space.

Methods to Call a Meeting

Call by the Chair

DEC Chairs may call General meetings with at least 10 days' written notice. Special meetings require at least 5 days' written notice, and the notice must clearly state the purpose.

Call by Petition

If a DEC Chair receives a written petition signed by at least 30% of total membership (excluding Automatic members), the Chair must schedule a Special meeting within 20 days, with at least 14 days' written notice to all members.

Failure-to-Call (Reorganization)

If the State Chair determines the DEC has failed to hold a meeting with quorum for six consecutive months, the State Chair may direct Congressional District Chair(s)/Vice Chair(s) to convene a reorganization meeting with at least 14 days' notice.

Establishing a Quorum

Per FDP Bylaws (Article I, Section 1.3.6), DEC general meetings have a quorum if 25% of the membership is present, excluding automatic members. Before the meeting is called to order, the chair should confirm quorum with the secretary or credentials committee.

Sample Meeting Agenda

  1. Call to Order
  2. Pledge of Allegiance
  3. Roll Call / Declaration of Quorum
  4. Approval of Agenda
  5. Approval of Minutes
  6. Officers Report (Chair, Vice Chair, Secretary, Treasurer, and/or State Committee)
  7. Old Business
  8. New Business
  9. Announcements
  10. Adjournment
💡 Guest speakers, local candidates, and celebrations of volunteer effort are excellent additions to any DEC meeting. Use your meeting to celebrate and motivate — people support that which they help to create.

Voting at Meetings

Voting shall be one (1) vote for each member. Per Article I, Section 4.1.1 of the FDP Charter, no vote shall be taken by secret ballot.

Proxies

Any member unable to attend may execute a written proxy and provide it to a DEC officer prior to the meeting. Incomplete proxies shall be returned to the member. The proxy holder shall be either another DEC member or a qualified Democrat who resides in the same precinct/district. No person shall hold more than one (1) proxy.

A proxy form is included in the Appendix of this manual.

Steering Committee

A Steering Committee supervises, administers, and carries out business between meetings of the DEC. The committee may take actions on behalf of the party only to the extent those actions are not inconsistent with applicable law, the FDP Charter and Bylaws, and the county DEC's bylaws. Decisions made by the Steering Committee should be ratified by the DEC at the next meeting.

The Steering Committee usually consists of the DEC officers, must include at least two State committee persons of different genders, and may include other members such as Sergeant at Arms, Parliamentarian, District leaders, Club and/or caucus presidents, Committee chairs, and Chair appointments.

County Clubs

Per FDP Bylaws Article VI Section 2, the charter of a Democratic club shall be entirely within the jurisdiction of the county Democratic Executive Committee where the majority of the membership resides. A county DEC shall not be authorized to establish a maximum number of Democratic clubs for the county.

For more information on chartering or running a County Democratic Club, contact [email protected].

County Caucuses

Local caucus chapters are chartered by State Caucuses. State Caucuses are under the jurisdiction of the FDP. For more information on chartering or running a county Democratic caucus, contact the appropriate State Caucus.

Democratic County Chairs Association (DCCA)

An independent organization created by the FDP to further the development of county DECs. Membership is composed of DEC Chairs and Vice Chairs (voting privileges reserved for dues-paying members). The DCCA promotes communication and exchange of ideas among chairs and vice chairs, and assists in furthering FDP objectives at the local, county, state, and national levels.

Small County Coalition (SCC)

A dependent organization created by the FDP for DECs in counties with fewer than 40,000 registered Democrats. Voting privileges are reserved for the Chairs, Vice Chairs, and State Committee Members of dues-paying counties. The SCC serves as the voice of all small counties to the FDP and holds monthly virtual meetings providing training, support, and encouragement.

Statutes and Bylaws Governing DECs

The Florida Statutes and FDP Charter & Bylaws govern all DEC activities. Key statutory references include Florida Statute 103.091 governing membership structure, and FDP Charter Articles I and V governing meeting rules, quorum, proxies, and officer duties.

Compliance is more than a box to check — it is the foundation of a well-run County Party. It means following all applicable state election laws, as well as the FDP Charter and Bylaws, while using best practices to maintain and govern the County Party.

Meeting compliance requirements involves more than understanding the rules; it means providing documentation to prove your County Party is operating transparently, legally, and ethically. Well-run organizations take compliance seriously because it signals to members, donors, and voters that the organization has integrity and can be trusted.

Risks of non-compliance: financial penalties, loss of trust among members and voters, damaged relationships with elected officials, potential social media backlash, State Party intervention, or worst-case scenario, loss of Charter.

Record Keeping

Documentation of DEC votes, actions taken, financial reports, membership lists, bylaws, and correspondence must be documented. It is recommended that all records be kept for at least 3 years, preferably in cloud/digital storage.

All ballots from elections must be kept for a minimum of 6 months and are reviewable by a DEC member upon written request. Inspection must be supervised by two DEC officers, then resealed. Both officers must sign, date, and note the time opened and resealed.

Part 3: Election Year Compliance

Non-Endorsement Statement — Due: April 1

A signed statement regarding the DEC's intention to endorse or not endorse candidates in a Primary must be sent to FDP by April 1 of the certifying year, as well as submitted to the County Supervisor of Elections' office. A non-endorsement statement is necessary to qualify for the DEC portion of any applicable candidate assessment and filing fees.

Citation: FLA STAT 103.121(4)(5); FDP Bylaws Article V, Sec. 3; FDP Bylaws Article 7, Sec. 7

Independent Expenditure Statement — Due: First Day of Qualifying (Second Monday of June)

The DEC Chair will send a notarized letter via certified mail to the Secretary of State. This Statement affirms the DEC has not made, and will not make, an independent expenditure in support of or opposition to any candidate through and including the upcoming general election.

Citation: FLA. STAT. Sec. 106.087(1)(a)

Campaign Plan (Election Budget)

A DEC receiving Party assessment fees shall submit a campaign/election budget plan detailing the expenditure of those fees to the FDP and the Budget and Finance Committee for approval.

Citations: FDP Charter Article V, Sec. 6; FDP Bylaws Article V, Sec. 6

In-Kind Contributions

An in-kind contribution is defined under Florida Statutes §106.011(3)(a) as a non-monetary gift of goods or services that has an attributable monetary value and is made to influence an election. There is no contribution limit on in-kind contributions to a State or County political party.

Examples

  • Professional services (polling, printing)
  • Office supplies, furniture, printers, paper
  • Rent of office space, postcards, stamps
  • Food, beverages, accommodations
  • Use of private aircraft or discounted campaign materials

Key Rules

  • Must be reported at fair market value
  • Must be itemized in the Treasurer's SOE report regardless of size
  • Must provide a direct benefit to the political party
  • Must be accepted before the contribution is made
Acceptance procedure: The Chair (or named designee on file with the SOE) must accept an in-kind contribution in writing before it is made. The donor must also provide written, signed, and dated notice of their intention to contribute.
⚠️ Warning: Any contribution received less than 5 days before an election may not be used on behalf of any candidate or party in that election. Any officer who aids or participates in a violation may be guilty of a felony with fines of $10,000–$50,000. State and County parties may not solicit contributions from religious, charitable, or civic organizations.

$50 Cash Rule

The DEC may accept cash donations from an individual in the aggregate of $50 per election. A cashier's check is treated the same as cash. Personal checks from a checking account are not considered "cash" under this rule.

Accepting aggregate cash or cashier's check contributions exceeding $50 per election is a misdemeanor of the first degree. Knowingly accepting more than $5,000 in violation is a felony of the third degree. Citation: FLA STAT. §106.09

$500 Threshold Rule (Clubs & Caucuses)

Under Florida Statutes §106.03, any group that receives contributions or makes expenditures over $500 in a calendar year for the purpose of influencing an election must register as a Political Committee (PC) and file regular campaign finance reports.

✓ Counts Toward $500

  • Donations to candidates, PACs, or political parties
  • Printing/distributing campaign literature endorsing/opposing a candidate
  • Running ads or mailings telling people how to vote
  • Hosting fundraisers with proceeds going to candidates
  • Coordinating GOTV for a specific candidate or ballot issue

✗ Does NOT Count Toward $500

  • Collecting membership dues
  • Paying rent or utilities for club meetings
  • Producing flyers marketing the club itself
  • Hosting purely social or educational meetings
  • Refreshments, postage, and internal admin expenses

Gambling Prohibition

DECs, Clubs, and Caucuses are prohibited from all gambling activities — including 50/50 raffles, regular raffles, bingo, and game/casino nights. This applies to all FDP entities.

Under Florida Statutes §849.0935, these are games of chance. Only 501(c)(3), (4), (7), (10), or (19) charitable organizations are eligible. DECs are tax-exempt under Section 527 and do not qualify. DEC Chairs must inform clubs under their jurisdiction, and compliance should be considered during club recertification.

Political Disclaimers

All materials — texts, emails, literature, slate cards, voter guides, radio, TV, and newspaper ads — created in support of or opposing a candidate or issue must include the required disclaimer. Exceptions: bumper stickers, buttons, hats, and t-shirts.

State Disclaimer

"Paid political advertisement paid for by the [County] County Democratic Executive Committee, [Address]"

Citation: FLA STAT. §106.143

Federal Disclaimer

"Paid for by [Committee Name]. Not authorized by any candidate or candidate's committee."

Both state AND federal disclaimers are required on all DEC websites — typically placed in headers or footers.

Election Ballots — Storage & Review

All ballots must be sealed and retained for six (6) months after the election. The Secretary must seal them immediately, dating, time-stamping, and signing the receptacle with a count of ballots inside.

Review Procedure

  • DEC members may submit a written request to review ballots within 6 months of the election
  • The Chair establishes a time, date, and location for in-person review; both parties may bring an observer
  • Ballots must remain in the room and not leave sight of the Secretary and Chair at any time
  • Upon conclusion, the Secretary recounts all ballots, reseals the receptacle, and signs/dates with the time opened and resealed

Opening a Bank Account & IRS Obligations

To open a bank account, a DEC must have its own Employer Identification Number (EIN), even without employees.

How to Obtain an EIN

  1. Go to IRS.gov EIN Online Application
  2. Select "View Additional Types" → choose "Political Organization"
  3. Select "To Open a Bank Account" as the reason
  4. Provide contact information and organization details
Donations vs. Income: Monies received by the DEC should be classified as contributions or donations — not income. Contributions from individuals, PACs, and in-kind donations are not taxable if used for political purposes. Report on IRS Form 8872 if required. Interest on bank accounts, rental income, and sales revenue from fundraising items (t-shirts, tickets) are considered taxable income.

Hosting In-Home Fundraising Events

Federal Candidates

An individual may spend up to $1,000 per candidate, per election on food, beverages, and invitations for an in-home event without it counting as a contribution. Each person living in the home gets a separate $1,000 allowance.

Florida (Non-Federal) Candidates

All money spent on food, beverages, and supplies for an in-home fundraiser for a Florida candidate must be reported as an in-kind donation to the candidate. The candidate must agree to receive and report the donation. Citation: FLA STAT. §106.025
